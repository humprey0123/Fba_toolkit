About This Research

This toolkit is the primary output of the study "Exploring the Level of Awareness of Pre-Service Special Needs Education Teachers on Functional Behavioral Assessment (FBA): A Basis for FBA Workshop." The research identified significant gaps in pre-service teacher knowledge of FBA concepts, data collection techniques, and intervention planning — gaps this workshop and toolkit are designed to address.

The study employed quantitative and qualitative methods, including a researcher-made survey instrument and Focus Group Discussions (FGDs) with 10 pre-service BSNEd teachers, yielding nine thematic findings that directly shaped every component of this toolkit.

Practice Scenarios

FBA Case Studies

Four evidence-based scenarios for hands-on practice with ABC data analysis and hypothesis formation. Expand each case to review full details.

🏫
Case 1 — Classroom Disruption
John, Age 10 · Grade 4 · Function: Escape
▼

A — Antecedent

Teacher gives John a math worksheet and asks him to complete it independently.

B — Behavior

John crumples the paper, throws it on the floor, and loudly says "I don't want to!"

C — Consequence

Teacher removes the worksheet and allows John to sit quietly until the next activity.
Hypothesis StatementWhen presented with independent written academic tasks, John engages in disruptive behavior (throwing materials, verbal refusal) in order to escape/avoid the difficult task.
Intervention IdeaProvide modified worksheets with fewer problems; offer task choices; use a break card system; reinforce task initiation and completion with short earned breaks; teach John to request help appropriately.
🏥
Case 2 — Self-Injurious Behavior
Maria, Age 8 · Therapy Room · Function: Escape Transition
▼

A — Antecedent

Therapist prompts Maria to transition from playing with blocks to a puzzle activity.

B — Behavior

Maria bangs her head lightly against the wall and cries.

C — Consequence

Therapist stops the puzzle activity and comforts Maria.
Hypothesis StatementWhen prompted to transition away from preferred activities (blocks), Maria engages in self-injurious behavior (head-banging, crying) in order to avoid transitions and maintain access to preferred items.
Intervention IdeaUse visual schedules with picture-based transition warnings; provide a 2-minute countdown before transitions; pair new activity with preferred items; reinforce successful transitions immediately with praise or brief return to blocks.
🏠
Case 3 — Attention-Seeking Behavior
Alex, Age 12 · Home Setting · Function: Attention
▼

A — Antecedent

Parents are talking to each other and not paying attention to Alex during mealtime.

B — Behavior

Alex throws food on the floor and shouts.

C — Consequence

Parents immediately scold Alex and focus full attention on him.
Hypothesis StatementWhen parents are not attending to Alex (mealtime inattention), Alex engages in disruptive behavior (throwing food, shouting) in order to gain immediate adult attention.
Intervention IdeaTeach Alex appropriate attention-requesting behavior (raising hand, using words like "Mom, can I talk?"); reinforce appropriate requests with immediate attention; minimize parental attention to disruptive acts (planned ignoring); schedule 1:1 family time proactively.
🎨
Case 4 — Sensory Avoidance
Liam, Age 7 · Art Class · Function: Sensory Escape
▼

A — Antecedent

Teacher gives Liam finger paints and asks him to create a picture.

B — Behavior

Liam screams, pushes the paint away, and covers his hands.

C — Consequence

Teacher allows Liam to use crayons instead.
Hypothesis StatementWhen presented with tactile/messy sensory materials (finger paint), Liam engages in avoidance behavior (screaming, pushing materials away) in order to escape uncomfortable tactile stimulation.
Intervention IdeaImplement a gradual sensory desensitization plan; begin with less-messy tools and introduce finger paint incrementally over sessions; pair sensory activities with preferred items as reinforcement; consult occupational therapist for sensory integration strategies.
